THE INSIGHT

What the examiner knows that you don't

Reading: "The passage always contains the answer. Always."▾
The examiner writes statements that use synonyms of passage words — so if you search for the exact statement words you won't find them. Look for meaning, not matching words.
Listening: "Every answer is said out loud. You will hear it."▾
The audio often gives you the wrong answer first, then corrects it. The last thing said on a topic is usually the answer.
Writing: "The examiner decides your band in the first paragraph."▾
Examiners spend about 2–3 minutes per script. Your introduction and topic sentences are read most carefully — they set the band before the examiner reads your arguments.
Speaking: "They're waiting to see if you can keep talking when you don't know the word."▾
If you don't know a word, describe it. Examiners reward communication strategies — working around a gap fluently scores higher than stopping and saying "I don't know".

IELTS OVERVIEW

What you need to know

STRUCTURE 4 sections, one sitting, approximately 3 hours total.
›
  • Reading and Listening are back to back with no break
  • Writing follows immediately after
  • Speaking is usually on a separate day at most test centres
  • You cannot go back to a previous section once time is called
LISTENING 30 minutes. You hear each recording exactly once — no replay.
›
  • You have 10 extra minutes at the end to transfer and check your answers
  • In the computerised test you type or click answers in real time as you listen — no transfer time needed
  • 4 recordings, 40 questions, increasing in difficulty
  • The biggest mistake: trying to understand every word. Listen for the answer, not the story.
READING 60 minutes, 40 questions across 3 passages of increasing difficulty.
›
  • Around 13–14 questions per passage
  • No extra time to transfer answers — what you see on screen is what gets marked
  • The passages are long and academic — you are not expected to understand every word
  • The biggest mistake: reading the whole passage before looking at the questions. Read the question first, then find the answer.
WRITING 2 tasks. Task 1 is 150 words minimum. Task 2 is 250 words and worth double the marks.
›
  • Task 1: you are given a graph, chart, table or diagram and asked to describe what you see in your own words
  • Task 2: you are given a statement or question and asked to write an essay arguing a position or discussing both sides
  • Most students spend too long on Task 1 and run out of time on Task 2 — Task 2 is worth double so always prioritise it
  • You will not be penalised for going over the word count but going under will cost you marks
SPEAKING 3 parts, 11–14 minutes, recorded in a private booth with an examiner.
›
  • Part 1: familiar topics like family, work, hobbies and daily routine — 4 to 5 minutes
  • Part 2: you are given a card with a topic and 1 minute to prepare a 2-minute talk
  • Part 3: a deeper discussion linked to your Part 2 topic — more abstract questions
  • The biggest mistake: stopping when you don't know a word. Keep talking, find another way to say it — that is exactly what fluency means
THE FORMAT The test is computerised — scroll passages, type essays, record Speaking in a booth.
›
  • Reading: scroll passages on screen, click your answers — no underlining on paper
  • Listening: audio through headphones, type or click answers in real time as you listen
  • Writing: type your essays — word count shown automatically
  • Speaking: recorded in a booth — no examiner in the room, just you and a microphone
  • You can copy/paste in Writing and use the highlight tool in Reading — use both
THE SCORES Your overall band is the average of all 4 sections, rounded to the nearest 0.5.
›
  • Each section is scored from 1 to 9. Most universities require Band 6.5 or 7.0 overall
  • Your overall band is the average of all 4 sections — one weak section drags everything down
  • Example: if you score 8, 8, 8 but only 5 in Speaking — your overall band is 7.0, not 8.0
  • The goal is not to be perfect in one section — it is to be consistent across all four
THE CRITERIA Every section is scored differently — knowing how changes how you prepare.
›
  • Reading & Listening: every correct answer = 1 mark. No partial credit, no penalty for wrong answers. If you're unsure, eliminate the obviously wrong options first and commit to your best remaining choice — a considered guess beats a blank every time
  • Writing: scored on 4 things — did you answer the question fully, is your essay easy to follow, do you use a range of vocabulary, and is your grammar varied and accurate. Task 2 is worth double Task 1 marks
  • Speaking: scored on 4 things — can you speak without long pauses, does your answer make sense and stay on topic, do you use a range of words, and how accurate is your grammar. Fluency matters more than perfection
THE INSIGHT Band 6 students answer what they think is right. Band 7 students find the evidence first.
›
  • Band 7 readers don't read faster — they read smarter. They scan for the answer before committing
  • Band 7 writers don't have better ideas — they have better structure. The examiner decides your band in the first paragraph
  • Band 7 speakers don't have perfect grammar — they keep talking when they don't know a word. That is what fluency looks like
  • The exam does not reward what you know. It rewards how you think under pressure
STARTING TODAY You're starting with Reading — the section most students underestimate.
›
  • Most students think Reading is their strongest section until they see their actual score
  • The passages are designed to trap confident readers — being good at English is not enough
  • The questions test whether you can find evidence, not whether you understood the passage
  • We start here because the thinking skills Reading builds carry over into every other section
