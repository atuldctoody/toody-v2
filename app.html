THE INSIGHT

What the examiner knows that you don't

Reading: "The passage always contains the answer. Always."▾
The examiner writes statements that use synonyms of passage words — so if you search for the exact statement words you won't find them. Look for meaning, not matching words.
Listening: "Every answer is said out loud. You will hear it."▾
The audio often gives you the wrong answer first, then corrects it. The last thing said on a topic is usually the answer.
Writing: "The examiner decides your band in the first paragraph."▾
Examiners spend about 2–3 minutes per script. Your introduction and topic sentences are read most carefully — they set the band before the examiner reads your arguments.
Speaking: "They're waiting to see if you can keep talking when you don't know the word."▾
If you don't know a word, describe it. Examiners reward communication strategies — working around a gap fluently scores higher than stopping and saying "I don't know".

THE FORMAT

The Computerised Test

The test is now mostly taken on computer at IELTS centres worldwide▾
Since 2019, most IELTS centres globally offer the computerised version. Results arrive in 3–5 days, versus 13 days for the paper-based test.
Reading: scroll through passages on screen, click to select your answers▾
Practise reading long text on screen — many students find it harder to concentrate than on paper. Train your eyes to scan efficiently, not just read linearly.
Listening: audio plays through headphones — you type or click answers in real time▾
Type fast enough to keep up — you can't pause or replay. In Toody's listening sessions, you'll train exactly this real-time response.
On screen: a highlighter tool for Reading and a word counter for Writing▾
Highlight key details in Reading passages as you read each question. The word counter removes the need to count manually — always aim for 150+ (Task 1) and 250+ (Task 2).
Speaking is recorded in a private booth — no examiner in the room▾
You're talking to a microphone, not a person. Some students find this easier. Others find it harder. Practise speaking aloud with Toody to get comfortable.

STARTING TODAY

You're starting with Reading

The most underestimated section — students assume they can already read▾
Students who read in English daily often assume they'll score well. They often don't — IELTS Reading tests precision, not general comprehension.
We start with True / False / Not Given because it's the trickiest question type▾
It appears in every Academic Reading test. A single confusion between False and Not Given can cost 2–3 questions — that's up to half a band.
The trap: "Not Given" ≠ False. The passage simply doesn't address the claim▾
False means the passage says the opposite. Not Given means the passage says nothing about it. This distinction alone separates Band 6 from Band 7 in Reading.
Most Band 6 students get this wrong because they read for meaning instead of evidence▾
They're reading for comprehension. The examiner tests whether you can tell the difference between silence and contradiction.
"Let's build the skill that most Band 6 students are missing."

THE SCORING

How Your Band Score Works

Each section scored 0–9 in 0.5 increments▾
There is no Band 0. Band 1 means non-user. Band 9 means expert. Most test-takers fall between 5.0 and 8.0.
Overall band = average of 4 sections, rounded to nearest 0.5▾
Unlike some exams, IELTS has no pass or fail — your score simply reflects your current English level. Institutions then set their own minimum requirements.
Example: 7.0 + 6.5 + 6.0 + 6.5 = Overall 6.5▾
The average is 6.5 here. If a section average fell between two bands (e.g. 6.375), it rounds to the nearest 0.5 — so 6.5 in this case.
Most universities need Band 6.5–7.5 overall with no band below 6.0▾
Always check your specific institution's requirements — some courses (like medicine or law) require higher minimum scores in individual sections.
Half a band can be the difference between an offer and a rejection▾
A 6.5 instead of 7.0 can close doors you don't know are closed. This is why Toody tracks your progress in 0.5-band increments.

THE CRITERIA

What Examiners Are Scoring

Reading + Listening: pure accuracy — every correct answer counts equally▾
Every correct answer earns exactly one mark. There's no partial credit, no bonus for difficulty. Speed and accuracy are both required.
Writing Task 1: Task Achievement, Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammar (25% each)▾
Task Achievement checks you described the data accurately. Coherence checks your paragraphs flow logically. Lexical Resource and Grammar check the quality of your English.
Writing Task 2: same 4 criteria — worth double Task 1▾
Spend roughly 40 minutes on Task 2 and 20 on Task 1. A weak Task 2 hurts your Writing band significantly more than a weak Task 1.
Speaking: Fluency, Vocabulary, Grammar, Pronunciation (25% each)▾
Fluency means speaking smoothly and at length — not just being correct. Vocabulary rewards range and precision. Pronunciation is clarity, not accent.
Examiners mark your English under pressure — not your knowledge▾
You can discuss topics you know nothing about and still score high. Examiners don't mark your ideas — they mark how you express them.
